About

Compassion is a vital and powerful tool that not only promotes positive emotional responses; but has been scientifically proven to benefit human physiology. Growing research has shown that the act of giving and receiving compassion is an excellent tool in calming physiological responses to stress and reducing patient mortality rates.

What is Compassion? According to Stephen Trzeciak in the text “Compassionomics”, Compassion is the emotional response to another’s pain or suffering, involving an authentic desire to help. (Trzeciak, 2019)

What is Physiology? Physiology is the study of the mechanism of living things. Research in this field aims to build an understanding of how the body operates when in health and when in disease. (What is physiology? 2025)

This website intends to highlight the important link between acts of compassion and positive physiological responses.

Peer-Reviewed Articles

Compassion and Mental Health (Nature)

Behaviors of compassion have been associated with improved levels of happiness and mental well-being, decreased inflammation, and lower risk of cardiovascular events. These physiological health benefits are also associated with reduced levels of loneliness that are mediated by compassion. Behaviors of compassion include: Compassion toward self (CTS) and Compassion toward others (CTO). Higher levels of CTO and CTS were found to be predictors of lower levels of loneliness. Lower levels of loneliness is correlated with improved health outcomes. On the other hand, high levels of loneliness are associated with higher levels of mortality. Performing behaviors of compassion towards oneself and others improves health outcomes through the reduction of loneliness.

The Impact of Compassion on Physiological Health (Springer)

Compassion involves a combination of empathy and action. To display compassion, one must feel concern for another's suffering and take action to alleviate it. High levels of compassion are linked to lower levels of diastolic and systolic blood pressure in adulthood and a lower risk for hypertension. Maintaining lower systolic blood pressure is significant because it is linked to a lower risk for cardiovascular events, such as coronary heart disease, stroke, and heart failure. High compassion is linked with a lower risk of eating-related problem behaviors, alcohol use, and smoking, all of which are risk factors for higher blood pressure.

Compassion and Stress Recovery Mechanisms (ScienceDirect)

Higher levels of compassion can lessen the physiological effects of stress. The physiological factors relating to stress include blood pressure and cortisol. Participants completed an online assessment of compassion, and were exposed to a social stress task. Their blood pressure and cortisol were monitored. Showing compassion for others shielded the individual’s physiological reactivity to stress. Higher levels of compassion were associated with lower blood pressure reactivity and lower cortisol reactivity.
